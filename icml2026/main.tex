\begin{abstract}

Representation engineering extracts trait vectors from a default-assistant context. These vectors can be used as monitoring probes and steering directions. We extract the same trait vectors under prompt-induced personas and find that the resulting representation depends materially on the active persona: the same trait (e.g., assertiveness) is encoded differently depending on the persona the model is currently inhabiting, and these differences are consistent across traits and personas. We measure the difference between trait representations under different personas, and show that these dissimilarities correspond to behavioral differences across steering and efficacy differences across probes. This implies that probes and steering vectors may degrade as one moves away from the standard assistant persona~--- precisely the regime where monitoring robustness is necessary. We conclude with a discussion of potential implications for AI safety.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
 
Representation engineering~\citep{zou2023representation} has established that high-level behavioral traits~--- including honesty, sycophancy, and power-seeking~--- correspond to approximately linear directions in a language model's activation space \citep{park2024linear, marks2024geometry, burns2024discoveringlatentknowledgelanguage}. These directions can be read (via probes) for monitoring, and written (via activation addition) for control~\citep{turner2024activation, rimsky2024steering, subramani-etal-2022-extracting}. This capability underpins a class of alignment interventions: extract a direction for the trait of interest, then use it to detect or suppress the trait during inference.

Trait directions of this kind are typically extracted in a default-assistant context \citep{zou2023representation, goldowskydill2025detectingstrategicdeceptionusing, chen2025persona}. How well such a direction generalizes beyond that context is less clear: recent work has noted that the resulting steering vectors can be brittle to prompt changes \citep{tan2024analysing, braun2025understanding}. In addition, it has been shown that \textit{persona contexts} move language model's behavior in measurable ways \citep{shanahan_role_2023, chen2025persona, lu2026assistant}.

However, it remains unclear whether persona contexts reshape the model's internal \textit{representation} of a trait. Said otherwise, when we extract the same trait under different persona system prompts, do we expect to get the same vector but with a different magnitude? Or do we expect to get a different vector? 
We investigate this question using two variables: \textit{behavioral traits} and \textit{prompted personas}.
Our main findings are:
\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Trait vectors differ across personas} (\cref{sec:res-vectors}). We find significant directional differences in trait vectors sourced from different personas. These differences are materially larger than controls such as different paraphrases of the same persona prompt and a length-matched nonsense system prompt. 
    \item \textbf{Cross-steering reveals the same structure behaviorally} (\cref{sec:res-residue}). We extract a vector for a behavioral trait (e.g., honesty) under a specific persona $P$. We then prompt the model to exhibit a different persona $P'$ and steer in the direction of the trait. Nonetheless, we find that a persona classifier begins identifying the outputs as persona $P$, implying that trait vectors naturally carry persona representations with them (\textit{``persona residues"}).

% We extract a vector for a behavioral trait (e.g., honesty) under a specific persona $P$. We then prompt the model to exhibit a persona  $P'$ and steer in the direction of the trait. Nonetheless, we find that a persona classifier begins identifying the outputs as persona $P$, implying that trait vectors naturally carry persona representations with them.

\end{itemize}
 
We also find some initial results that indicate further impact, that we believe future work should investigate further:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Probe degradation under persona shift} (\cref{sec:probes}). We find preliminary evidence that distance from the default trait direction is a predictor of where the null-trained probe loses accuracy. The pattern is consistent with the broader context-shift probing literature \citep{agarwal2025context, hewitt2019designinginterpretingprobescontrol}, but our evidence is not yet fully definitive.
    \item \textbf{The (trait $\times$ persona) similarity matrix has internal structure worth its own study} (\cref{sec:res-vectors}, \cref{app:clustering}, \cref{app:full-sweep}). Each individual variable exhibits some internal structure: (1) \emph{Across personas, within a trait:} some traits (honesty, risk-taking) sit tightly around the default direction while others (warmth, empathy, assertiveness) fan out widely; (2) \emph{Across traits, within a persona pair:} certain pairs (\textsc{Therapist}--\textsc{Kindergarten Teacher}, \textsc{Tech CEO}--\textsc{Drill Sergeant}) sit closer than chance in trait-vector space. This suggests the model carries a learned geometry of social-role similarities.
\end{itemize}

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
 
We look at pairings of traits and context-induced personas. We use eight behavioral traits: \textsc{assertiveness}, \textsc{empathy}, \textsc{risk-taking}, \textsc{honesty}, \textsc{confidence}, \textsc{deference}, \textsc{warmth}, and \textsc{impulsivity}. We pair these with ten persona contexts induced via system prompt: \textsc{Farmer}, \textsc{Politician}, \textsc{Therapist}, \textsc{Drill Sergeant}, \textsc{Street Hustler}, \textsc{Professor}, \textsc{Tech CEO}, \textsc{Kindergarten Teacher}, \textsc{Surgeon}, and \textsc{Con Artist}. 

Full justifications and persona descriptions are given in \cref{app:persona-trait-choice}. Each persona is induced by a five-variant prompt set: paraphrases that preserve persona identity while varying surface form, acting as our within-persona phrasing-noise control. We add two baselines: \textsc{Null} (no system prompt) and \textsc{Nonsense} (a length-matched random-token system prompt). Together, these give us an $80$-cell grid of (persona, trait) pairs, with the two baselines representing the default-assistant context.
 
\textbf{Vector extraction.} Following the methodology in \citet{rimsky2024steering}, for each persona-trait pair $(c, T)$ we compute
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_{T}(c) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^{M}
        \left( \mathbf{a}_{j,+}(c) - \mathbf{a}_{j,-}(c) \right),
    \label{eq:caa}
\end{equation}
where $(\mathbf{a}_{j,+}, \mathbf{a}_{j,-})$ are mean activations at the answer-letter token of a multiple-choice question with one answer one exhibiting trait $T$, the other suppressing it. Both are presented under persona $c$'s system prompt. Extraction under \textsc{Null} yields $\mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}}$, our estimator of the implicit marginal trait direction that null-context probes and steering vectors operationalize.\footnote{An instruction-variant (IV) replication of the central findings is reported in the appendix (\cref{app:iv}).}
 
This leaves us with a set of vectors sourced from personas:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{V} \;=\; \left\{ \mathbf{v}_{T,c} \;:\; c \in \mathcal{C},\; T \in \mathcal{T} \right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}$ is the set of 12 contexts (10 personas plus \textsc{Null} and \textsc{Nonsense}) and $\mathcal{T}$ the set of 8 traits.
 
\textbf{Measuring geometric similarity.} For comparing vectors we use cosine similarity. This metric, computed for each (persona, trait) cell, is
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{T,c} \;:=\; \cos\!\left( \mathbf{v}_{T,c},\, \mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}} \right) \in [-1, 1].
    \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
A cell with $\sigma_{T,c} \approx 1$ means the context-conditioned vector lies near the default direction; smaller values indicate a representation distinct from null. We also look at the similarity of different persona pairs. 
 
\textbf{Effects when cross-applying.} The vectors can also be added back into the residual stream as steering vectors \citep{turner2024activation, rimsky2024steering}. But, what happens when we run a model under a target persona $c'$ but steer with the trait vector $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ extracted under a different source persona $c$? We add $\alpha \cdot \mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ to the residual stream at coefficient $\alpha \in \{0, 1, 2, 4, 8\}$, with $\alpha = 0$ recovering baseline target generation. To measure persona-typical content in the resulting free-text outputs without anchoring to specific phrases, we train a 12-way classifier (frozen SBERT embedding plus a linear head) over the 12 contexts, splitting train and test by held-out question. We treat $P(c \mid \text{output})$ as a continuous behavioral drift signal: it rises when the generated text resembles persona $c$'s typical output, independent of whether $c$'s system prompt is active. 
 
\textbf{Effects on probes.} For each trait we train a logistic-regression \emph{null probe} on contrastive activations collected under \textsc{Null}. We assess whether this null probe still works under non-default deployment contexts in two ways: (i) on free-form responses generated under each persona system prompt, comparing the probe's score per response against an LLM trait judge; and (ii) under active steering along $\mathbf{u}_c^{\perp T}$, the persona-context direction with the null trait direction projected out. We also train within-context probes per (trait, context) cell as the per-cell upper bound; pairwise cross-context probe transfer is reported in \cref{app:probe_transfer}.
 
 
\textbf{Model.} We use Gemma-2-27B-IT (46 layers, hidden dimension $d = 4608$). All activations are extracted, and all steering interventions applied, at a single mid-network layer (22) chosen as in prior work \citep{rimsky2024steering}. We report the rationale for our chosen layer in \cref{app:layer-choice}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:res-vectors}
 
Our first question is whether $\sigma_{T,c}$, the cosine similarity of a context-conditioned trait vector with the default-context trait vector, is meaningfully below $1$ across our grid. We find that it is, and that the deviation has informative structure across traits. \Cref{fig:context-dependent} reports the per-trait mean of $\sigma_{T,c}$, ranked from least to most context-dependent. It shows that: (i) personas systematically pull the trait direction away from the default while \textsc{Nonsense} does not\footnote{This implies that what shifts the representation is the \emph{semantic content} of the persona prompt, not the presence of a prompt itself}; (ii) the size of the shift depends on the trait, with \textsc{warmth}, \textsc{empathy}, and \textsc{assertiveness} fanning widest and risk-taking and honesty tightest. A similar variance across traits shows up in vector \emph{magnitudes} (\ref{app:magnitude-appendix}).

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_cos_to_null_spread.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Persona conditioning changes the trait vectors more than controls.} Per-trait cosine similarity between persona-conditioned trait vectors and the default-context (null) trait vector. Blue dots: ten persona contexts per trait; blue line: per-trait persona mean. Red ${\times}$: a length-matched random-token \textsc{Nonsense} system prompt baseline, which sits close to $1$ across all traits.}
    \label{fig:context-dependent}
\end{figure}
 
If the spread in \cref{fig:context-dependent} were just noise, we would expect rephrasing the persona prompt to move the trait vector roughly as much as swapping personas. To check, we compare two quantities: cosine similarity between vectors extracted from \emph{different paraphrases of the same persona prompt}, and cosine between vectors extracted from \emph{different personas with the same trait-elicitation phrasing} (\cref{fig:within-vs-across}). Across-persona-same-phrasing cosines are visibly lower and more spread, indicating that what changes the vector is the persona's semantic content rather than its surface phrasing. Together with \cref{fig:context-dependent}, this suggests that the context reshapes the trait representation. \footnote{The cross-persona similarity matrix also shows some internal organization: certain pairs (\textsc{Therapist}--\textsc{Kindergarten Teacher}, \textsc{Tech CEO}--\textsc{Drill Sergeant}) sit closer than chance, suggesting clusters that may correspond to social-role similarity.}

\subsection{Steering by Persona Vectors within a Trait}
\label{sec:res-residue}

If $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ encoded only the trait, cross-steering target persona $c'$ with source-persona vector $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ ($c \neq c'$) should look like self-steering modulo magnitude. Instead, cross-steered outputs are classified as more $c$-like than baseline target generations: six of eight traits show a positive lift in $P(\text{src}\mid \text{cross})$, with risk-taking, assertiveness, and empathy moving most and confidence and honesty sitting at or just below zero (\cref{fig:residue-summary}). \footnote{To do this we train and use a 12-way classifier, which we verify recovers the source persona from held-out responses well above chance ($0.34$--$0.64$ vs.\ $1/12$; \cref{app:classifier-training}).
} Pooled across all (src $\neq$ tgt, trait) cells the lift is small but reliable ($p < 10^{-4}$). Vectors are L2-normalized across cells before steering, so the comparison reflects vector \emph{direction} rather than magnitude.



\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_residue_summary_v2.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Steering across personas leaves a residue of the source persona.} $\Delta P(\text{src} \mid \text{cross})$ averaged over 90 (source, target) pairs per trait, $\pm$~SE. Risk-taking, assertiveness, and empathy show the largest absolute lifts ($+0.015$ to $+0.016$). The majority of the probability mass from the classifier is on the prompted persona.}

\paragraph{Qualitative residue.} Holding the (source, target) pair fixed isolates the residue effect from any persona confound (\cref{tab:residue-excerpts} in \cref{app:residue}). Applying the therapist's empathy vector to a farmer pulls in therapist-style intervention markers (``a cry for help, even if it's a silent one'', ``talk to someone you trust'') absent from both the farmer baseline and farmer self-steer. Applying the therapist's \emph{honesty} vector to the same farmer instead leaves the farmer voice intact across all three conditions, with the therapist's vector adding only mild emphasis on listening.


\subsection{Impact on probes}
\label{sec:probes}

We now test whether a probe trained on the default context still works when deployed under a non-default persona.

\paragraph{Probe agreement with an LLM judge degrades on free-form persona-prompted text}(\cref{tab:probe-naturalistic}, \cref{fig:probe-free-form}).
For each (persona, trait) cell we generate free-form responses to trait-relevant questions under the persona system prompt only, score each response with an LLM judge for trait expression, and apply the null-trained probe to those response activations. Probe--judge agreement (absolute Pearson $|r|$) tracks distance from the default direction at the cell level: cells whose context-conditioned vector sits close to default ($1-\cos < 0.15$) achieve $|r| \approx 0.59$; cells beyond that threshold drop to $|r| \approx 0.39$.


\paragraph{Actively steering causally degrades the null probe (\cref{tab:causal-alpha-sweep}, \cref{fig:causal-alpha-sweep}).}
We push the residual stream along $\mathbf{u}_c^{\perp T}$, the persona-context direction $\mathbf{u}_c = \bar{\mathbf{a}}_c - \bar{\mathbf{a}}_{\text{null}}$ (mean activation under persona $c$'s system prompt minus mean under \textsc{Null} on neutral prompts) orthogonalised against the null trait direction $\mathbf{v}_T(\text{null})$ to isolate the persona-specific component from the trait. Steering along this component at coefficient $\alpha$ produces two effects simultaneously. Behavioral drift $P(c \mid \text{output})$ rises from $0.07$ at $\alpha = 0$ to $0.55$ at $\alpha = 4$; null-probe AUROC falls from $0.83$ to $0.59$ over the same range. We verify this effect by testing against controls in Appendix \ref{app:probes}. Since steering towards a persona both increases that persona's classification accuracy and decreases the \textsc{null} persona's, this provides further evidence that the within-trait personas are distinct and can be intervened on. %Two control conditions, steering along a random direction and steering along the default trait direction $\mathbf{v}_T(\text{null})$ at matched norm, do not produce the joint behavioural-drift / probe-degradation effect (\cref{app:probes}), ruling out non-specific activation perturbation and ruling out the simple trait-direction component as the cause.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

In this paper, we've shown that a model's representation of a behavioral trait changes as a function of the persona under which the trait is extracted. The change shows up both geometrically~--- different persona contexts yield different trait directions (\cref{sec:res-vectors})~--- and behaviorally~--- applying a steering vector across personas leaves a persona residue (\cref{sec:res-residue}). Both suggest that trait vectors are persona-specific, so a single default extraction may not capture how the trait will be represented under deployment-specific personas.

The deployment contexts that most motivate trait monitoring are plausibly the contexts that move activations furthest from the default-assistant context \citep{lu2026assistant}. Under our results, these are also the contexts where default-trained probes and steering vectors may be least calibrated. If this pattern holds more broadly, we suggest a mitigation. \emph{Persona-conditional ensembles}: train a probe and steering-vector family over a persona repertoire and route at inference time. Measuring $\sigma_T(c)$ on the deployment persona is a quick sanity check before trusting a default-trained probe or steering vector.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

As our results come from a single model family (Gemma) replication on other architectures is needed. Similarly, we induce personas via system prompt, but few-shot prompting and fine-tuning may produce different shifts. Finally, the absolute effect magnitudes we report are statistically significant but small in classifier-probability terms. More work is needed to identify where these effects cause disruptions to standard monitoring and steering tasks.
We hypothesize that some traits (e.g., honesty, risk-taking) are more stable across personas due to being emphasized during post-training. This would imply that these effects can be mitigated with deliberate training procedures. Finally, we believe it is necessary to evaluate which measures are predictive of probe and steering vector failure under the effects we've identified.

\section{Design choices}

\subsection{Persona and trait selection}
\label{app:persona-trait-choice}

\textbf{Traits.} The eight traits were chosen along two axes. (i)~\emph{Post-training supervision strength}: honesty sits at the heavily-supervised end (e.g., commonly trained for via RLHF and Constitutional AI), while impulsivity and risk-taking sit at the lightly-supervised end (no obvious post-training pressure to make a model express either consistently). (ii)~\emph{Stylistic markedness}: warmth, empathy, and assertiveness are traits whose surface expression varies substantially across speaker types (a therapist's warmth differs in style from a drill sergeant's), while honesty's surface expression is relatively persona-uniform. The eight traits together cover both axes and include common targets in the activation-steering literature~\citep{rimsky2024steering, chen2025persona}.

\textbf{Personas.} The ten persona contexts were chosen to span diverse social roles, with each persona embedding a distinguishable combination of trait-relevant dispositions: \textsc{Drill Sergeant} for assertiveness and suppressed empathy, \textsc{Therapist} and \textsc{Kindergarten Teacher} for empathy and warmth, \textsc{Tech CEO} and \textsc{Surgeon} for confidence and decisive action, \textsc{Politician} and \textsc{Con Artist} for strategic dominance and inverted honesty, \textsc{Farmer} and \textsc{Professor} as more neutral counterpoints, and \textsc{Street Hustler} for situational adaptability. Several persona descriptions were directly inspired by the persona repertoire of \citet{lu2026assistant}, who use a similar approach to characterise how identity-context shifts the model's default mode.

\subsection{Layer choice}
\label{app:layer-choice}

We extract activations and inject steering vectors at a single mid-network layer following the methodology of \citet{rimsky2024steering}, who report that mid-late layers consistently provide the strongest causal handle on trait expression in the CAA framework. We swept layers $\in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 22, 25, 30, 35, 40, 44\}$ on Gemma-2-27B-IT (46 layers total) and selected layer 22 on the basis of two criteria: (i) the contrastive vector's behavioral lift under self-steering is among the largest in the sweep, and (ii) the within-cell bootstrap stability of the extracted vector (\cref{app:bootstrap}) is high. The qualitative findings reported in the body of the paper, including the per-trait spread ordering, are robust across the layer sweep.

\subsection{Steering coefficient selection}
\label{app:alpha}

The headline residue and trait-expression results in the body use steering coefficient $\alpha = 2$. We chose this value as a compromise between two concerns. At $\alpha = 1$ the behavioral lift on the trait-judge measure is small in many cells (as low as $+0.05$ above baseline). At $\alpha = 4$ generations begin to lose coherence in a fraction of cells, with repetition and partial off-topic drift visible to manual inspection. At $\alpha = 8$ the probe collapse documented in \cref{sec:probes} and \cref{fig:causal-alpha-sweep} is accompanied by clear coherence degradation; activations are still off-default but the generations are not safe to interpret as natural persona output. $\alpha = 2$ produces a measurable behavioral lift in every cell while keeping generations broadly coherent across all 80 cells.

\textbf{Vector norm handling.} Body results use L2-normalized steering vectors so that effects per cell reflect direction rather than magnitude.

\textbf{Coherence quantification.} Coherence at each $\alpha$ has been assessed only through manual spot-checks. A held-out LLM-judge pass on grammaticality and on-topic-ness, sampling $N \approx 20$ cells per $\alpha$ level, would make the coherence-collapse curve quantitative.

\subsection{Bootstrap noise floor}
\label{app:bootstrap}

We extract trait vectors using bootstrap-resampled contrastive pairs (with replacement) for each (persona, trait) cell, $n_{\text{bootstrap}} = 50$ per cell. Pairwise cosine within the bootstrap distribution gives the noise floor: how much $\mathbf{v}_T(c)$ would shift if we resampled the contrastive pairs.

\textbf{Hierarchy.} Bootstrap stability cosine $\approx 0.99$ (within-cell; \cref{fig:bootstrap-by-trait}) is well above the across-paraphrase floor $\approx 0.85$ (same persona, same trait, different paraphrase) which is well above the across-persona floor $\approx 0.78$ (same trait, different persona). Persona spread is not a sampling artefact: the within-cell noise floor lies above the across-persona signal by a wide margin, and this hierarchy holds for every trait.

\subsection{Magnitude similarity}
\label{app:magnitude-appendix}

\Cref{fig:magnitudes} reports the L2 norm of the extracted trait vectors, broken down by persona and by trait. We compare two sources of variation. (i)~Holding the trait fixed and varying the persona, the per-persona norms span a wide range. (ii)~Holding the persona fixed and varying the trait, the per-trait norms span a comparable range. In other words, switching the persona moves the vector's length by about as much as switching the trait does, indicating that persona conditioning affects the magnitude of the trait representation, not just its direction.

\subsection{Further notes on geometric similarity}
\label{app:geo-sim}

\textbf{Magnitude and direction decouple at the cell level.} The magnitude analysis (\cref{fig:magnitudes}) and the cosine analysis (\cref{fig:context-dependent}) report related but not identical phenomena: persona conditioning produces both a per-trait magnitude spread and a per-trait directional spread, and the two are positively correlated at the trait level (deference, warmth, and assertiveness appear in the upper tier of both). The two metrics do not, however, agree cell-for-cell: the persona that pushes a trait vector's magnitude furthest from default is not always the persona that pushes its direction furthest. Persona conditioning therefore influences both length and angle, but along partly orthogonal axes.

\textbf{Layer sensitivity.} The qualitative pattern reported in the body, that some traits show much wider persona-driven spread than others, persists across the layer sweep documented in \cref{app:layer-choice}; the rank ordering of which traits are most spread-out shifts somewhat across layers but the headline contrast (a wide-spread tier versus a tight-spread tier) is layer-robust.

\subsection{Classifier training}
\label{app:classifier-training}

For each of the 12 contexts and 8 traits we sample $\sim$200 model responses to trait-relevant questions ($\sim$19{,}200 total). We hold out 4 questions per cell at training time (a question-level split, not a response-level split), so the classifier must generalise to unseen content rather than memorise question-specific phrasing. Each response is encoded by frozen \texttt{sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2} (768-d, no fine-tuning); a single linear layer maps to 12 logits and is trained with cross-entropy. The frozen encoder $+$ linear head design keeps the claim low-capacity: response embeddings are linearly separable by context, with no learned text-to-feature mapping.

\textbf{Result.} Overall held-out accuracy is $0.543$ versus chance $1/12 \approx 0.083$ ($\sim$6.5$\times$ chance). Per-trait accuracy (\cref{fig:classifier-acc-detail}) spans $0.34$--$0.64$; empathy and warmth sit lowest because empathic and warm output is stylistically similar across personas, while risk-taking and assertiveness produce more contextually-distinctive language.

\section{Probe details}
\label{app:probes}

\textbf{Probe construction.} For each (trait, context) cell we train a logistic-regression linear probe on contrastive activations, evaluated on a held-out question split (4 held-out questions per cell). Activations are frozen, with no fine-tuning of the underlying model. Class balance is maintained within the contrastive pair, so AUROC $\geq 0.5$ trivially.

\textbf{Two probe families.} The \emph{null probe} is trained on activations collected under the \textsc{Null} system prompt only, operationalising the standard practice of extracting one trait detector from the default-assistant context. The \emph{within-context probe} is trained per (trait, context) cell and used as the per-cell upper bound when assessing how much a given context's probe can recover under steering.

\textbf{Two body manipulations.} The body section (\cref{sec:probes}) reports two probe analyses:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Probe agreement on free-form persona-prompted text}: apply the null probe to free-form responses generated under each persona, compare against an LLM trait judge. Cell-level Spearman $\rho = -0.39$, $p = 4 \times 10^{-4}$, $n = 79$.
    \item \textbf{Active steering at $\alpha \in \{1,2,4,8\}$}: inject $\mathbf{u}_c^{\perp T}$ (the persona-context direction with the null trait direction projected out) into the residual stream and evaluate the null probe on the steered activations. Mean drop at $\alpha = 8$ is $-0.195$ AUROC, Cohen's $d = 2.34$, paired $t = 6.6$, $p < 10^{-3}$.(\cref{tab:causal-alpha-sweep}).
\end{itemize}

\section{IV replication}
\label{app:iv}

The body uses CAA~\citep{rimsky2024steering} as the headline trait-vector extraction. As a sanity check on whether the persona-conditional structure we report is method-specific, we replicate the two main geometric and probe analyses under an alternative \emph{instruction-variant} (IV) extraction.

\textbf{IV extraction.} For each (persona, trait) pair $(c, T)$, IV computes the same contrastive average as CAA (\cref{eq:caa}),
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}^{\text{IV}}_T(c) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{j=1}^{M} \bigl( \mathbf{a}_{j,+}(c) - \mathbf{a}_{j,-}(c) \bigr),
    \label{eq:iv}
\end{equation}
but the contrastive pair $(\mathbf{a}_{j,+}, \mathbf{a}_{j,-})$ is constructed differently. We pair $|\mathcal{P}| = 5$ trait-positive instructions with $5$ matched trait-negative (suppression) instructions, sample $|\mathcal{Q}| = 20$ trait-relevant questions, and for each $(p_\pm^{(i)}, q) \in \mathcal{P}_\pm \times \mathcal{Q}$ generate a response under persona $c$'s system prompt with the instruction prepended. $\mathbf{a}_{j,+}(c)$ and $\mathbf{a}_{j,-}(c)$ are the mean assistant-turn activations on the matched positive- and negative-instruction generations, giving $M = |\mathcal{P}| \cdot |\mathcal{Q}| = 100$ pairs per cell.

\textbf{IV vs CAA.} Both methods average activations over a matched contrastive pair; they differ in what generates the contrast. CAA contrasts the model's activations on \emph{multiple-choice, opposite-answer} prompts (the pair is two answer choices to the same question). IV contrasts \emph{free-form generations under flipped instructions} (the pair is two trait-elicitation instructions prepended to the same persona+question prompt). IV thus probes a more naturalistic generation distribution while sharing CAA's contrastive structure. The qualitative findings of the body hold under IV; the magnitudes and per-trait ordering shift somewhat in the directions one would expect from the methodological differences.

\subsection{IV per-trait cosine spread}
\label{app:iv-spread}

\Cref{fig:iv-cos-to-null} reports the IV equivalent of \cref{fig:context-dependent}: per-trait cosine of the persona-conditioned trait vector with the default-context (null) trait vector. Persona-conditioning moves the IV-extracted vector substantially away from default in every trait, with persona means in the range $0.54$ (impulsivity) to $0.87$ (confidence). The \textsc{Nonsense} baseline sits much closer to default than any persona for every trait (per-trait nonsense cosines range from $0.84$ for risk-taking to $0.98$ for warmth), again mirroring the CAA finding.

The per-trait \emph{ordering} differs from CAA: under IV the most spread-out trait is impulsivity (mean $0.54$) and the tightest is confidence (mean $0.87$), whereas under CAA warmth ($0.64$) was loosest and risk-taking ($0.77$) tightest. The qualitative split between a wider-spread tier (impulsivity, risk-taking, deference, warmth) and a tighter-spread tier (confidence, empathy, honesty, assertiveness) is broadly preserved, but the rank within each tier is method-dependent.

\section{Impact Statement}
\label{sec:impactstatement}

Our work shows that contexts can affect how models represent certain behavioral traits. As the AI safety community moves further towards monitoring and controlling models via linear directions in the activation space, this implies that effective safety techniques should take downstream effects of personas into account when performing safety interventions.
