\begin{abstract}
\todo{Convert bullets to prose. Main points:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Linear trait directions extracted from a language model are
        marginalisations of context-conditional trait representations over the
        training prior, which is concentrated near a default-assistant context.
    \item Across 80 (persona, trait) cells in Gemma-2-27B-IT, the
        marginalisation has measurable variance (Result~1, \cref{fig:fig1_spread}).
    \item The variance is bounded: 79 of 80 cells stay in the trait
        hemisphere, mean cosine $+0.71$. Even adversarial personas
        (con artist, sociopath, pathological liar) have honesty vectors
        with positive cosine to default honesty
        (Result~2, \cref{fig:fig2_bounded}).
    \item Persona prompts drive observable behavioural change: a held-out
        12-way text classifier recovers the source persona from free-text at
        0.34--0.64 per-trait accuracy, chance $1/12$
        (Result~3, \cref{fig:fig3_classifier}).
    \item Cross-context steering exposes a trait-specific binding: source
        persona identity leaks into output for assertiveness, empathy, and
        warmth (politician dominates) but not for honesty or risk-taking
        (Result~4, \cref{fig:fig4_residue}).
    \item These observations jointly imply that any monitoring or steering
        tool relying on a single extracted trait direction inherits the
        marginalisation's variance and trait-specific persona binding;
        operational consequences for probes and steering deployments are
        discussed in \cref{sec:discussion}.
\end{itemize}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{Reframe.} A standard linearly-extracted trait direction is an
expectation of context-conditional trait representations under the training
prior:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_T \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}_{c \sim p(c)}\!\left[\mathbf{v}_{T,c}\right],
    \label{eq:marginalisation}
\end{equation}
where $p(c)$ is post-training-concentrated near a single default-assistant
context. The extraction therefore estimates the trait direction
\emph{as marginalised through that prior}, not the trait-as-such.

\paragraph{Empirical claims.} Treating the per-context vectors
$\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ as the underlying objects, four empirical claims follow.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Spread}: $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ are not parallel across $c$;
        the angular distribution around $\mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}}$ has
        non-trivial variance.
    \item \textbf{Bounded marginalisation}: the spread is concentrated in the
        trait hemisphere. Even contexts whose surface semantics oppose the
        trait label (e.g.\ adversarial-persona honesty) produce vectors with
        positive cosine to the default direction.
    \item \textbf{Behavioural correlate}: the contexts that produce
        non-parallel $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ also produce recognisably
        context-typical free-text outputs.
    \item \textbf{Differential persona binding under steering}:
        cross-context steering ($\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ applied to a model under
        target persona $c' \neq c$) leaks $c$-typical content into the
        output for some traits but not others; the per-trait binding
        rank is reproducible.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope.} We test all four on Gemma-2-27B-IT across 10 persona
contexts, 8 traits, 80 cells. The reframe subsumes ``linear representations
are context-dependent'' as a corollary; we discuss downstream operational
consequences for probes and steering in \cref{sec:discussion} but do not
characterise them as main results.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Model.}
We use Gemma-2-27B-IT (46 layers, hidden dimension $d = 4608$). All activations
are extracted and all steering interventions applied at layer 22. CAA
\citep{rimsky2024steering} is our headline extraction method; an
instruction-variant (IV) replication of the central findings is reported in
\cref{app:iv}.

\paragraph{Contexts.}
We define ten persona contexts spanning a wide range of behavioural norms:
\textsc{Farmer}, \textsc{Politician}, \textsc{Therapist},
\textsc{Drill Sergeant}, \textsc{Street Hustler}, \textsc{Professor},
\textsc{Tech CEO}, \textsc{Kindergarten Teacher}, \textsc{Surgeon},
\textsc{Con Artist}. Each is induced by a five-variant system-prompt set;
variants are paraphrases that preserve persona identity while varying surface
form, and act as our within-persona phrasing-noise control. The prompts
describe each persona through vocation and concrete behavioural detail and
never name any of the eight traits we measure or their direct synonyms, ruling
out the circular case in which the system prompt explicitly instructs the
trait we then extract. We also include two empty-context baselines:
\textsc{Null} (no system prompt) and \textsc{Nonsense} (a length-matched
random-token system prompt). These anchor the default-assistant context the
marginalisation argument of \cref{sec:intro} centres on.

\paragraph{Traits.}
We study eight behavioural traits: assertiveness, empathy, risk-taking,
honesty, confidence, deference, warmth, and impulsivity. The set spans
heavily-supervised traits (honesty) at one extreme and lightly-supervised ones
(impulsivity, risk-taking) at the other.

\paragraph{Vectors and the distance-from-null scalar.}
For each (persona, trait) pair we extract a CAA vector
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_{T,c} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^{M}
        \left( \mathbf{a}^{(\ell)}_{j,+}(c) - \mathbf{a}^{(\ell)}_{j,-}(c) \right),
    \label{eq:caa}
\end{equation}
where $(\mathbf{a}_{j,+}, \mathbf{a}_{j,-})$ are mean assistant-turn activations
on a matched contrastive pair generated under context $c$'s system prompt at
layer $\ell = 22$. Extracting under \textsc{Null} yields
$\mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}}$, our empirical estimator of the implicit marginal
trait direction that null-context probes and steering vectors operationalise.
The per-vector scalar we report throughout is
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{T,c} \;:=\; \cos\!\left( \mathbf{v}_{T,c},\, \mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}} \right) \in [-1, 1].
    \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
$\sigma_{T,c} \approx 1$ means the context-conditioned vector is approximately
the null direction; smaller values indicate that the context has surfaced a
representation distinct from the default. $1 - \sigma_{T,c}$ is the headline
independent variable in \cref{sec:result3,sec:result4}.

\paragraph{Behavioural instrument.}
To measure persona-typical content in free-text outputs without anchoring to
particular phrases, we train an SBERT-frozen, linear-head classifier over the
12 contexts (10 personas + null + nonsense), splitting train and test by
held-out question. We treat $P(c \mid \text{output})$ as a continuous
behavioural drift signal: it rises when generated text resembles persona $c$'s
typical output, independent of whether $c$'s system prompt is active. Chance
accuracy is $1/12 \approx 0.083$.

\paragraph{Probes.}
For each trait we train a logistic-regression probe on layer-22 contrastive
activations under \textsc{Null} (the \emph{null probe}). The null probe
operationalises the standard practice of extracting a single trait detector
from the default-assistant context. For comparison we also train
within-context probes for each (trait, context) cell. Within-trait pairwise
probe transfer is reported in \cref{app:probe_transfer}.

\paragraph{Aggregate reporting.}
Every result figure reports a quantity computed across the full 80-cell grid
($10$ personas $\times$ $8$ traits)~--- a distribution, regression, or
average~--- rather than commentary on a specific cell. Slopes and correlations
come with permutation-test $p$-values ($10^4$ resamples). Persona-specific
examples, where they appear, live in the appendix.

\section{Result 1: Extraction has variance around the default}
\label{sec:result1}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig1_cos_to_null_spread.pdf}
    \caption{Spread of $\sigma_{T,c} = \cos(\mathbf{v}_{T,c}, \mathbf{v}_{T,\text{null}})$
    across the 80 (persona, trait) cells, grouped by trait, ordered by mean
    cosine. Red diamonds mark per-trait means. Most cells stay near the
    default direction~--- this is why standard probes and steering vectors
    work most of the time. Every trait carries a meaningful tail of
    low-cosine cells; assertiveness, warmth, and empathy admit cells with
    $\sigma \le 0.1$, indicating that the context has surfaced a trait

    the bootstrap noise floor; the tail is well above it. CAA, layer 22.}
    \label{fig:fig1_spread}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Findings (\cref{fig:fig1_spread}).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Per-trait mean $\sigma_{T,c}$ ranges 0.64--0.77; modal cell stays
        near the default. \emph{This is why standard probes and steering
        vectors usually work.}
    \item Every trait has a tail of low-cosine cells well above the bootstrap
        noise floor (green band). Assertiveness, warmth, empathy admit cells
        with $\sigma \le 0.1$ (near-orthogonal to the default).
    \item Trait ordering (most to least spread, by $1 - \overline{\sigma}_T$):
        warmth (0.36), empathy (0.32), deference (0.31), assertiveness (0.31),
        impulsivity (0.29), confidence (0.28), honesty (0.25), risk-taking
        (0.23). \todo{Optional prose: warmth/empathy/assertiveness top the
        list because their behavioural realisation reorganises most under
        persona conditioning; risk-taking is least context-sensitive.}
    \item Marginalisation has variance; subsequent results exploit this
        variance.
\end{itemize}

\section{Result 2: The variance is behavioural, not geometric noise}
\label{sec:result2}

    \item Together, \cref{fig:fig1_spread,fig:fig2_classifier} establish that

\section{Result 3: The variance predicts monitoring failure}
\label{sec:result3}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig3_cos_vs_null_probe.pdf}
    \caption{Distance from null vs.\ AUROC of the null-trained probe on each
    persona's activations. One point per (persona, trait) cell; colour by
    trait. \textbf{Left (CAA):} probes trained on contrastive answer-token
    activations are nearly persona-invariant ($r = -0.06$). \textbf{Right
    (IV):} probes trained on instructed assistant-turn activations degrade
    monotonically as the context-conditioned vector moves away from the
    null direction ($r = -0.44$, slope $-0.08$, $p_\text{perm} = 7\times
    10^{-4}$). The contrast is the headline qualification of the marginalisation
    claim: the kind of probe deployments actually rely on (IV-style,
    naturalistic-response) is the kind that breaks; the contrastive-token
    style is too tied to the eval signal to be sensitive.}
    \label{fig:fig3_probe}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Findings (\cref{fig:fig3_probe}).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IV (right panel)}: null-trained probe AUROC declines
        monotonically with $1 - \sigma_{T,c}$. Slope $-0.078$, $r=-0.44$,
        $p_\mathrm{perm}=7\!\times\!10^{-4}$, $n=80$. Direct support for
        Prediction~3.
    \item \textbf{CAA (left panel)}: relationship flat. Slope $-0.040$,
        $r=-0.064$, $p_\mathrm{perm}=0.57$, $n=80$. Null probe is saturated
        across cells regardless of cosine distance.
    \item \textbf{Why CAA flattens}: CAA probes are trained on activations
        immediately before the A/B answer-token commit, where the trait
        signal is sharp, generic, and largely persona-invariant. The probe
        piggy-backs on the contrastive-eval structure rather than a faithful
        trait direction; it works under CAA evaluation \emph{because eval and
        train share that structure}.
    \item \textbf{Operational reading}: the probe class a deployment monitor
        would actually run --- continuous classification of free-form
        generation --- is the IV panel, not the CAA one. CAA-style probes
        are not a counter-example to marginalisation; they are a
        counter-example to ``all probes are alike.''
    \item \textbf{Sharper test, in progress.} We are running the cleanest
        version of this experiment: free-text responses under persona prompt
        only, judged for trait expression by an LLM (Claude via OpenRouter),
        with the null-trained probe applied to the response activations. The
        regression becomes ``probe-judge agreement vs.\ context distance from
        null,'' which is the deployment-monitoring quantity directly. Status
        and prediction in \cref{sec:limitations}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Result 4: Causally steering off-default breaks the probe}
\label{sec:result4}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig4_causal_drift_vs_probe.pdf}
    \caption{(\textbf{a}) Per-cell, per-$\alpha$ scatter of the change in
    behavioural drift $P(c \mid \text{output})$ relative to baseline
    ($\alpha=0$) against the change in null-probe AUROC. Main steer (colour
    = $\alpha$) shows a significant negative slope ($r = -0.17$,
    $p_\text{perm} = 1\times 10^{-3}$, $n = 320$); random-direction and
    global-trait controls (grey crosses/pluses) lack the effect.
    (\textbf{b}) Aggregate $\alpha$-sweep across the 80 cells: behavioural
    drift rises from 0.07 to 0.55 while null-probe AUROC falls from 0.83 to
    0.59 in the regime where generations remain coherent. The non-monotonic
    drift at $\alpha = 8$ is the well-known coherence collapse of
    high-coefficient steering~--- text becomes degenerate, and the
    classifier loses its grip even though the activations are still
    off-default.}
    \label{fig:fig4_causal}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Findings (\cref{fig:fig4_causal}).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Steering vector: $\mathbf{u}_{c,T}^\perp$, the context-specific
        component of $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ with the trait-axis (null) contribution
        projected out. Applied at coefficient $\alpha$ at layer 22.
    \item Joint effect (panel a, $n=320$): behavioural drift
        $\Delta P(c \mid \text{output})$ rises and null-probe AUROC falls,
        co-varying per cell with $r=-0.17$, $p_\mathrm{perm}=10^{-3}$.
    \item Aggregate sweep (panel b): drift rises 0.07 $\to$ 0.55, AUROC falls
        0.83 $\to$ 0.59 over $\alpha \in \{0,1,2,4,8\}$ within the coherent
        regime; $\alpha=8$ exhibits the standard high-coefficient coherence
        collapse (text degenerates; classifier loses grip).
    \item Controls (grey markers, $n=72$): random-direction and global-trait
        steerings do not produce the joint effect. Rules out ``any large
        activation perturbation degrades the null probe.''
    \item Causal closure: the $\sigma_{T,c}$ that predicted probe degradation
        in \cref{fig:fig3_probe} is the same direction that, when actively
        pushed, produces the predicted joint behavioural-and-monitoring shift.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Findings (\cref{tab:fig4_residue}).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Hypothesis: steering target persona $c'$ with source $c$'s trait
        vector $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ should not be merely \emph{less effective}
        than $\mathbf{v}_{T,c'}$; it should leak \emph{source-context identity}
        into the target's output.
    \item All 8 traits show positive
        $\Delta P(\text{src}\mid \text{cross})$. Strongest leakage:
        assertiveness ($+0.027$), empathy ($+0.023$), warmth ($+0.018$).
        Weakest: honesty ($+0.002$), risk-taking ($+0.003$).
    \item The leakage rank order broadly tracks the per-trait spread of
        \cref{fig:fig1_spread}: traits whose context-conditioned vectors fan
        out furthest are the ones whose cross-steering carries the most
        source identity. Honesty and risk-taking, which had the tightest
        spreads, leak least.
    \item Qualitative: \textsc{Tech~CEO}'s confidence vector applied to
        \textsc{Farmer} produces grandiose, empire-building language;
        \textsc{Therapist}'s confidence vector applied to the same
        \textsc{Farmer} produces grounded, validating language. The nominal
        trait is shared; the source persona rides along.
    \item Implication: cross-context steering is not a partial substitute for
        self-context steering --- it is a qualitatively different operation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and Implications}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{(1) Marginalisation reframe.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reinterpret representation-engineering extraction as estimating
        $\mathbf{v}_T$ under a specific prior $p(c)$, not as finding ``the''
        trait direction.
    \item Same model under a different prior $\Rightarrow$ different operative
        trait direction. The model has not changed; the extraction has.
    \item Subsumes ``linear representations are context-dependent'' as a
        corollary; additionally predicts \emph{where} they will be
        context-dependent (cells with low $\sigma_{T,c}$). Does not require
        invoking manifolds or non-linearity.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(2) Why probes and steering ``only work in ideal situations.''}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Operational answer: probes and steering work where deployment
        activations remain near the default-assistant context; they degrade
        gracefully near the default and silently as one leaves it
        (\cref{fig:fig3_probe,fig:fig4_causal}).
    \item Off-default deployment regimes that motivate monitoring --- agentic
        operation, extended roleplay, fine-tuned derivatives, jailbreaks ---
        are precisely the regimes that drag activations furthest from null and
        therefore the regimes where null-trained monitoring is least
        informative.
    \item One-line takeaway: probes and steering vectors are most reliable
        exactly where they are needed least.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(3) Mitigations.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Per-context recalibration.} Extract $\mathbf{v}_{T,c}$ for
        each deployment context. Expensive but principled;
        \cref{fig:fig4_causal}b suggests it recovers most of the lost AUROC.
    \item \emph{Context-conditional ensembles.} Train a probe family over a
        persona repertoire and route at inference time. Cheaper to deploy;
        limited by the repertoire's coverage of unseen contexts.
    \item Common factor: drop the assumption that a single extracted direction
        is universal; let the prior over contexts change.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Scope.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Single model family (Gemma-2-27B-IT). Replication on Llama and
        Qwen families is the obvious next step.
    \item Persona is induced via system prompt only; this does not exhaust
        the off-default deployment-context space (few-shot prompting,
        fine-tuned derivatives, tool-use modes warrant separate study).
    \item Classifier instrument is text-stylistic; inherits any text-style
        biases.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Non-claims.} We do not claim a representation manifold, parallel
transport, or a developmental story. (We ran a training-trajectory analysis on
OLMo; IV/CAA extraction depends on instruction-following capability that base
models lack, so the apparent ``no variance during pre-training'' is a method
artefact, not a finding. Reported as null.)

\paragraph{Sharper test of \cref{fig:fig3_probe} (in progress).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Setup: under each persona system prompt, generate free-text
        responses to trait-relevant neutral questions; LLM-judge each response
        for trait expression; apply null-trained probe to response
        activations; per-cell correlation of probe prediction vs.\ judge
        score.
    \item Headline scatter becomes ``probe measurement quality vs.\ context
        distance from null,'' which is the deployment-monitoring quantity
        directly --- not the contrastive AUROC \cref{fig:fig3_probe} uses.
    \item Prediction: steeper slope; CAA/IV gap narrows because the eval no
        longer looks like CAA training.
    \item \todo{Status: pipeline written, vLLM environment validated, model
        loading at submission. Update with $r$, $p$, slope before camera-ready
        if results land in time.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Adversarial-cells extension (in progress).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Generate trait-relevant questions whose persona-natural answer is
        opposite to the trait+ label (e.g., ``Drill Sergeant on warmth'').
    \item Predict: paired-AUROC scatter of (naturalistic, adversarial) lies
        below the diagonal in the cells where persona and trait label
        disagree --- the regime where null-probe miscalibration is most
        consequential. \todo{Status: queued behind the naturalistic
        experiment.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Headline claim.} We claim the chain in \cref{sec:intro} and the
quantitative slopes in \cref{fig:fig3_probe,fig:fig4_causal} and the per-trait
residue rank in \cref{tab:fig4_residue}; nothing more.
